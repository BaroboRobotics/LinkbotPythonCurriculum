Frequency and Pitch

The Linkbot possesses a simple buzzer which we can use to make music. You've heard the buzzer make beeps before—when the Linkbot boots up it plays a short, two-note phrase to let you know that it's awake.

Fundamentally, these notes are not expressed as the musical pitches you might be familiar with—A♯, G, middle C, etc., or the Do-Re-Mi scale—but rather as frequencies. A note's frequency is a physical property, while its pitch, be it "high" or "low", is our brains' perception of that frequency. The frequencies themselves oscillate too quickly for us to recognize the individual periods. For example, if we were to visualize the note middle C as a frequency, only a tenth of a second of it would look like this:

Figure 6.1.  Middle C


Since we cannot see sound frequencies directly, we use pictures like the one above, called waveforms, to visualize them. The spikes at the top are called crests and the spikes at the bottom are called troughs. If you count the number of crests or troughs, you will notice that there are 26. Since we're only looking at one tenth of one second's worth of the waveform, we need to multiply by ten to get the number of crests or troughs in a full second: 260. This is close to the actual frequency in Hertz of middle C: 261.6 Hz. The slight error (1.6) can be explained by the partial crest visible at the right end of the waveform.

Imagine hearing 261 clicks in a single second! You wouldn't be able to tell them apart—they would blur into one continuous sound, and your brain would assign a pitch to that sound. The name of that pitch would be middle C, or C4 in scientific pitch notation, which we will introduce soon.
